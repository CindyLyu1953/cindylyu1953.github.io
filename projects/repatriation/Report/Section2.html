Uncovering the Patterns of Repatriation: A Multi-Lens Exploration

Geospatial

The dispossession of Indigenous lands was not just an act of removal-it redrew the very map of the United States. In this section, we explore the geospatial story of repatriation: how different lands, once seized through acts like the Morrill Act, now bear the responsibility to return Native ancestors and heritage.

Beginning with an examination of historical land transfers, and moving into an analysis of state-level repatriation efforts today, we trace how geography shapes both the burdens of the past and the possibilities for justice.

Geospatial - by Land Origin

Figure 2.1 - Morrill Act Land Area by State

This interactive choropleth map visualizes the land area (in acres) granted to each state under the Morrill Act, which played a pivotal role in shaping the foundation of many land-grant universities. By hovering over a state, you can view the state code along with the specific land area awarded.

Morrill Act Land Area by State

The color gradient highlights significant disparities in land allocations. Darker shades indicate states that received a larger amount of land. Notably, California, Wisconsin, Nebraska, Michigan, Minnesota, and Kansas stand out with the most substantial Morrill Act land holdings. These patterns reveal the geographic concentration of lands seized from Indigenous peoples to fund educational institutions—an important historical context for understanding universities’ heightened ethical responsibilities in current repatriation efforts.

Starting with the lands ceded under the Morrill Act, we first examine where dispossession laid its deepest roots. From there, we shift our focus to the present, explore how repatriation efforts vary across modern state lines. By comparing historical land seizures with contemporary acts of return, we glimpse both continuity and change in the ongoing journey toward restitution.

Geospatial - by State

Figure 2.2 - Repatriation Trends by State

This interactive visualization presents the annual trends of repatriated items by U.S. state. You can explore the repatriation activity of any of the 50 states by selecting from the dropdown menu. The chart includes a main plot with a range slider view below, enabling users to dynamically adjust the time window and focus on specific historical periods. Because the y-axis is fixed across all states, you can also make meaningful comparisons between state-level performances. This design invites both targeted exploration and broad comparison, helping audiences uncover disparities in institutional responsiveness at the state level.

Repatriation Trends Across U.S. States

Top 3 Performing States: California, Massachusetts, and New York

California, Massachusetts, and New York emerge as the top three states in terms of cumulative repatriation activity. All three show noticeable spikes in recent years, particularly in 2022–2024, aligning with the nationwide surge following the updated NAGPRA regulations.

  • California leads by a wide margin, peaking at over 160 repatriations in 2024. As the state with some of the largest museum and university collections—including UC system campuses and cultural institutions—California’s leadership reflects both historical accumulation and recent pressure to take action.

  • Massachusetts shows an earlier spike around 2001, suggesting a longer history of institutional engagement, but its recent activity also increases sharply, likely involving major universities and museums based in Boston.

  • New York has a steadier but upward trend, consistent with its large academic and museum landscape.

These three states highlight how institutional concentration, public scrutiny, and local Indigenous advocacy may jointly shape repatriation outcomes.

Broader Trend: Limited Progress in Most States

Despite the progress seen in a few leading states, the majority of U.S. states show minimal or stagnant repatriation activity throughout the timeline. In many cases, annual counts remain in the single digits or zero. This reflects persistent institutional inertia, under-resourced inventories, or even complete inaction in states with smaller collections or fewer regulatory pressures.

States Showing Some Progress: Washington and Wisconsin

While not in the top three, states like Washington and Wisconsin demonstrate modest yet visible efforts. Washington exhibits a steady climb starting in around 2006, peaking around 2013 and again in 2024. Wisconsin presents a scattered pattern with minor peaks in 2014, 2018, and 2022. These examples highlight that regional actors can make meaningful progress, though consistent commitment remains rare.

Figure 2.3 - Total Repatriation by State

This interactive choropleth map provides a geographic overview of repatriation activity across all 50 U.S. states. By hovering over each state, you can view its name, state code, and total number of repatriated items in detail. The map serves as a visual complement to the time-series line chart, allowing for quick comparison of cumulative totals at a glance.

Total Repatriation by State

Unsurprisingly, California stands out with the darkest shading, corresponding to about 2.4 million repatriated items—largely due to recent large-scale returns by institutions like Sacramento State. Most other states remain relatively light in color, highlighting disparities in institutional engagement across the country.

Figure 2.4 - Pending Repatriation by State

This interactive map visualizes the number of pending repatriation cases across U.S. states, with options to toggle between Individuals, Funerary Objects, and Total counts. By hovering over a specific state, users can view the state name and the exact pending item count. This functionality enables a detailed examination of where the unfinished work of repatriation remains most urgent.

Pending Repatriation by State

When viewed alongside the total repatriation map, a clear contrast emerges. Ohio stands out as a notable example—despite some repatriation activity, the state still holds an exceptionally high number of pending returns, far exceeding what has been completed. States such as Washington, Illinois, Pennsylvania, and California, while having demonstrated significant repatriation efforts, continue to retain substantial numbers of unrepatriated remains and cultural objects.

This discrepancy reveals that many institutions have made only partial efforts, leaving thousands of ancestors and cultural items in limbo. The map underscores a broader challenge: repatriation is not simply about symbolic gestures, but requires sustained, systematic commitment to justice and healing.

Our geospatial analysis reveals both encouraging progress and persistent disparities in the repatriation of Native American ancestors and cultural items.

While a few states—such as California, Massachusetts, and New York—have demonstrated leadership in returning items, many others have shown only minimal activity over time. In numerous states, the volume of unrepatriated individuals and objects continues to exceed what has been returned. Even in states with visible efforts, significant unfinished obligations remain.

Taken together, these visualizations highlight a key insight: progress has been uneven, and historical injustices persist both in scale and geography. Repatriation is not a task completed, but an ongoing responsibility that demands sustained institutional accountability.
